Storytelling is something you hear referred to all the time when it comes to the theory of Environmental/Science communication. But just saying that your communication strategy should incorporate storytelling elements isn’t really enough, we need to understand what that means and what impacts different kinds of stories can have. That’s what this module will be all about.

Shapes of Stories

We can also imagine stories not just as a series of happenings to and decisions by a protagonist, but alos in terms of the emotional highs and lows explicit in the story.

Kurt Vonnegut popularized the idea that we can think about the shapes of stories. Watch the short lecture below where he describes this theory.

In addition, read this article in The Atlantic summarizing this paper wherein the author’s tried to quantify the theories of story shape laid out by Kurt Vonnegut and come to pretty similar conclusions.

Writing for Voice

Remember, good writing is good writing regardless of the format. There’s more similar than different between writing for radio and writing for other mediums. That said, here are some tips for effective radio writing.

  • Write like you speak: to make it personal and intimate, you have to sound like a real person using real words and expressions that people use in everyday life. One way to do this when writing a radio piece is just to talk about your topic to a friend and record what you say (including the bits where they ask you to explain what you mean when you use complicated language). You canthen transcribe that and use it as a basis for the kinds of language and sentence structure they might use for your story. Its not about mimicking your natural tenancies exactly, its more about using these to design this “best version” of you for radio.

  • Use short sentences: run-ons, big clauses, subject a long way from the start of the sentence - all of these things make listening to someone difficult. Keep it to one idea per sentence. In the end, this is not bad advice for writing more generally anyway.

  • Use concise, vivid imagery: You can’t use pictures so use the audiences imagination to generate scene and a sense of place. Tap into common imagery and use the diversity of peoples imaginations to your advantage.

  • Write in a logical simple order: The listener can’t go back and reread what you wrote so if they get confused along the way then they’ll stay that way.

  • Grab their attention: You wont always have everyone’s full attention – listeners are often driving, doing the dishes, having a conversation etc. You have to grab peoples’ attention and then sustain it by laying out a story in an entertaining and logical manner. Sign-posting can also be effective.

  • Make it personal: Radio is like having someone talking directly to you. Remember to write like you are talking to just one person and not an audience-filled room.
